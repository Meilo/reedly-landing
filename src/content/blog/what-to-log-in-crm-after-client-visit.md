You're back in your car. The meeting went well. You've got three pages of scribbled notes, two business cards, and a clear next step agreed with the client. You open your CRM on your phone, stare at the empty fields, and think: "What do I actually need to log here?"

Most reps either dump everything (three paragraphs nobody will read) or log almost nothing (just the date and "good meeting"). Neither works. Here's what actually matters.

## The 8 details that make a CRM entry useful

**1. Next action with a date**  
Not "follow up soon." Write "Send proposal by March 15" or "Call back week of April 3 to confirm budget." If there's no next action, the visit didn't accomplish anything.

**2. Decision-maker names and roles**  
You met three people. Write down who makes the final call, who influences it, and who'll be your day-to-day contact. Six months from now, you won't remember that "Christine from ops" is actually the budget owner.

**3. One specific pain point**  
Not "they need better efficiency." Write "they're manually re-entering data from Excel into their ERP, takes 4 hours/week." Specific pain = specific solution later.

**4. Budget and timeline reality check**  
Did they confirm a budget exists? When do they actually need this solved? "We're looking into it" and "we need this by Q2" are two very different situations.

## What not to waste time logging

Don't transcribe the entire conversation. Don't log what the weather was like. Don't write a novel about the office decor.

Your CRM entry should answer one question: **"If I'm sick tomorrow and someone else needs to pick this up, what do they absolutely need to know?"**

That's your filter. Everything else is noise.

Good CRM data after a visit isn't about volume, it's about clarity. Log the 8 essentials right after the meeting, while it's fresh. Your future self (and your manager) will thank you.
